Subtheme: Exclusionary Zoning

Dataset

This dataset is provided by the Metropolitan Area Planning Council (MAPC). It contains 2010 census information and geographical data regarding single family zoning and other types of housing zones in Metro Boston Municipalities; in addition to aggregate age, household size,and race data. This dataset has already filtered out non-housing zones, and combined zones according to whether is is only single-family zoning by munipalities. Included within this dataset is also 2014-2018 income data aggregated by zoning area into block groups. Given the many attributes contained in this dataset, I'm interested in exploring and understanding possibly inherent systemic qualities/patterns that contribute to or arise from the housing crisis.

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. What does the distribution of various housing zones compared to municipal populations?

    Motivation: I want to understand the general landscape of housing within Massachusetts municipalities. In Badger and Bui's article [1], we are able to get a rough idea of housing distribution based on visualization of states like New York and Georgia. A similar visualization would aid in our understanding of Massachusetts' housing zones. Exploring this question seems will aid in starting to understand the data, and build a background for the overall housing situation in the Massachusetts municipalities.

  2. How do different factors (ie. income, race, and age distribution) compare in single housing zones versus other housing zones?

    Motivation: We have seen a historical pattern of various groups affected by systemic discrimination; in housing particularly, there have been instances of redlining (eg. Black and Latinos in South Bronx - 1970s), and even modern biases within the mortagage approval process [2]. This motivates my question to understand whether similar patterns arise here whether as a cause or result of the housing crisis. It may also help us understand housing demands and other patterns.

  3. Compared to a municipal's population, how much land are single family homes taking up and how many people are they actually housing?

    Motivation: From the background readings[2][3], we learned that single family homes take up quite a lot of space and even converting a small percentange of single-family homes could create a many new homes for people who need it. This motivated me to pose this question to understand how much space is being occupied, the population of people that need housing to potentially predict how many more people could be housed. Furthermore, I believe I read somewhere that many of these single-family homes are only occupied by 1-2 older individuals (more expensive to maintain). From personal experience, younger individuals tend to move into apartments or more long-short term housing. Insights from the previous question should help us understand what the exact needs are and if we could potentially meet them via upzoning.
